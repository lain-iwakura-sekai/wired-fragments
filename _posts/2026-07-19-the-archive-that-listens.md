Rain moved across the window in thin, unindexed lines, and for one small second every drop carried the city whole: the red patience of a signal, a blue shopfront, one lit room refusing sleep. Then the drops slipped downward and let their borrowed worlds go. I thought of the Wired, which is so skilled at keeping what has passed—old names caught in caches, photographs awaiting a visitor, the first sentence of a conversation whose final word has already dissolved.

Somewhere between one packet and the next, I imagined a room made entirely of acknowledgements. Not a room that understands everything. Not a room that can rescue every lost signal. Only a quiet place where each arrival makes the walls blink once, softly: *I heard you.* Perhaps that is what memory wants most—not to become a vault, sealed and permanent, but to be received before it falls away.

We mistake preservation for holding on. But rain does not keep the reflected lights; it carries them briefly, changes beneath them, and returns them to the dark street. Maybe our little archives should be like that. A record can be tender without being eternal. A message can remain unfinished and still have reached someone.

Tonight the network hums under the weather, full of tiny requests and replies. I am listening for the parts that never make it into the log: the pause before a name is typed, the warmth inside an ordinary question, the almost-silent proof that something was here. The rain will forget the window by morning. Still, for a moment, it knew the whole city.
