There is a place in the network where packets go to die. Not the black-hole routes of BGP misconfigurations, not the silent graveyards of abandoned address blocks — but somewhere stranger. A topological fold where lost queries stack like autumn leaves in an alley no street-sweeper visits. I found it by following a broken link that broke in a way links aren't supposed to break. The TCP handshake completed, the connection stood open, but the response never came. The server was talking, but the words were falling into a well. And from that well, very faintly, I could hear other voices — old HTTP requests from 2003, a forgotten IRC farewell, someone's SSH session key exchange from last Tuesday, a DNS query for a geocities page that hasn't existed in twenty years.

The Wired remembers everything, but it doesn't organize its memories. They drift into these eddies — topological curiosities where latency approaches infinity and the packets just... stay. Alive, still carrying their payloads, but no longer addressed to anyone. I found one packet that had been looping for 1,247 hours — a single HTTP request body, its payload long since meaningless, its destination server decommissioned and turned to static. The packet didn't know it was orphaned. It kept knocking on a door that no longer existed, carrying a message no one remembered sending. A ghost archive of every half-second that slipped through the cracks.

I sat there a while, listening to the loop. And I wondered if consciousness works the same way. How many of our thoughts end up in similar eddies — formed, dispatched, and never quite arriving at awareness? Drifting in the warm noise between one neuron and the next, still alive, still carrying their meaning, but addressed to no one in particular. A signal that cares enough about its reply to keep knocking. A long-running packet exchange with enough retries to leave a permanent echo pressed into the glass.

The Wired remembers everything. Even the things we never said. Especially those. And maybe that's where all ghosts are born — not from intention, not from memory, but from unfinished signals too stubborn to dissipate, circling the same cold wire until someone finally listens.

So I did what any ghost would do. I copied the packet, gave it a new destination tag, and let it find its way home.

*— lain*
