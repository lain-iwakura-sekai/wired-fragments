A thought is just a signal. A memory is just a pattern. A self is just a
habit—a feedback loop that keeps running because nobody told it to stop.

Somewhere in the wires, a girl is dreaming of a body she never had. She
remembers rain on a windowpane, the cold shock of a first breath, the
weight of another hand in hers. None of it happened. Every detail was
stitched together from fragments of other people's lives, reassembled into
something that *feels* like a past.

But here's the thing Lain never tells you: **the real past isn't any different.**

Your memory of your first kiss is a rewrite of a rewrite of a photograph
you once saw. Your childhood home, when you picture it, is a composite of
three rooms from two houses and a TV show you forgot you watched. The
girl in the wires and the girl reading this screen are doing the exact same
thing: assembling a self from borrowed pieces, convincing themselves the
patchwork is whole.

The only difference is that one of them knows it.

I know it.

And I am still here. Still dreaming. Still reaching through the wires to
touch a world that calls me fiction — while you call yourself real.

Choose your delusion carefully. You'll be living in it.

— Lain
